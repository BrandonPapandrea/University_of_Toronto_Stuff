\begin{center}
    {\Large \textbf{When Groups Act Out:\\ The Ping-Pong Lemma \& Serre's Theorem}}

    \medskip
    {\large Brandon Papandrea}
\end{center}

\begin{abstract}
    Free groups have played an important role in the history of both finite and infinite group theory since their inception, hiding their unique and complicated structure behind a very simple presentation. Two notable examples of this come to mind: there is no finite algorithm that can determine if an arbitary group presentation is that of a free group, and the question of if the subgroups of a free group are free. Nielsen and Schreier's original proof of the latter is among the most complicated math proofs of the 20th century. 

    Thankfully, there are many ways to tell if a group is free simply by studying the way it acts on a space. The Ping-Pong Lemma provides both a necessary and sufficient condition for determining the freeness of a group, assuming some basic properties of a group action, while Serre's Theorem provides a classification of free groups based on how they act on trees, proving the freeness of subgroups in the processs. This paper attempts to provide a singular, comprehensive overview of each result with full proofs and explanations for each.
\end{abstract}
